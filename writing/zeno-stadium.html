Zeno's Paradox of the Stadium

In this essay I argue that Zeno's Paradox of the Stadium cannot be resolved by refutation of the atomic theory of space and time on the basis of Weyl's theorem. The steps in my argument for this thesis are: 1) I will present the most charitable version of Zeno's Stadium paradox which assumes the atomic theory of space and time(ATST) and the orthodox theory of motion.2)I will present solution to the Stadium paradox by refutation of atomic theory of space and time 3) I will present the Weyl's argument for refutation of ATST 4)I will consider McDaniel's argument against Weyl which states that there is no justification to reject ATST. 5) I will conclude that Weyl's theorem fails to reject ATST and cannot be used to solve the Stadium paradox.

1) Zeno's paradox of the Stadium

The Stadium paradox has two main interpretations. The first interpretation, however, is not charitable. It shows that Zeno could forget to take into account the knowledge of relative motion. Therefore, I present the second interpretation which relies on the atomic theory of space and time (ATST).This is the theory according to which:

1. Time can be subdivided into instants of equal but non-zero duration, within which no change occurs.

2. Any bounded stretch of time contains at most finitely many such instants

3. Any line can be divided into points of equal but nonzero magnitude, which are themselves indivisible

4. Any bounded segment of a line contains at most finitely many such points

The paradox can then be summarized as follows: There are three rows containing one body each. The bodies are: A, B and C. Each of the bodies occupies a single atomic point of the lines where they are located. Assume the orthodox theory of motion (OTM). In our situation A is stationary, C is moving to the left and B is moving to the right. Blocks are moving at the same speed. For many units of time is A lined up with B or C? For how many units of time is B lined up with C? In order to answer these questions I apply the method used by J Immerwhar as it avoids drawing diagrams.

I. AuB=t is the number of atomic units of time when A is lined up with B.

II. AuB=2(BuC) from the relativity theory it follows that since A is stationary B passes C with twice the speed it passes A. Therefore, the time B is lined up with A is twice the time B is lined up with C.

III. AuB=AuC Because B and C pass A with same speeds the time they are lined up with it is the same.

IV. AuC=t from I and II.

V. BuC=t If B and C are both lined up with A for the time interval t, they are also lined up with each other for this time interval.

VI. AuB=2t from V and II.

VII. t=2t.

We get a contradiction that two atomic units of time are equal to one. This is the conclusion of the Stadium paradox.

2)

I now examine the paradox without presupposing ATST. It turns out that the issue is not paradoxical anymore. To show this I use Cantor theory of infinite sets and the notion of zero. One of Cantor's theorems states that all infinite numbers satisfy the equality 2x=x. It is important to point out that under ATST all possible x numbers will be finite and the equality is false. Once I put ATST aside, I can apply the theory. If x is the number of instants of time that pass during the motion of the body B it can be equal to twice the number of instants of time that pass during the motion C. Another solution is that in continuous time the blocks are never actually lined up with each other; they pass by each other. Therefore the instant when they are lined up is the time of no duration numerical value of which is zero. We don't get a paradox as 2*0=0. Therefore, to avoid the paradox we need infinite numbers or zero which is not allowed under ATST.

3)

My objection now concerns the ATST. What is the justification for presupposing it? Herman Weyl has an argument which states that such space structure is impossible. In the following argument I call the space with properties which are true under ATST-discrete.

1. Space is discrete if and only if every finite extended region of space is composed of finitely many atomic regions of space

2. Pythagorean theory states : "the length of the square of the hypotenuse is the sum of the squares of the length of two other sides"

3. When the space is discrete the hypotenuse is the same number of units long as each of the other sides of the triangle

4. The Pythagorean theorem fails to hold in worlds with discrete space

5. Pythagorean theory must hold (or at least approximately hold) in physical space

6. Physical space is not discrete

It follows that the ATST is impossible to be true in our physical world. It cannot be presupposed in the construction of the Stadium paradox, the solution to which is then given in section 2.

4)

It does not seem persuasive to me that Weyl's argument can lead to simple exclusion of ATST form the Stadium paradox in order to get out of the paradoxical issue. I present McDaniel's argument which shows that Weyl's argument is false and provides a counter example to his argument. I can see an "appeal to consequences" fallacy in Weyl's argument. In premise 2, 3 it is presupposed that the "length/size of any region of discrete space is the number of points that the region has as parts/units". McDaniel calls this the "size thesis" (ST). He gives a counterexample to the tile argument in the paper "Distance and Discrete Space" which shows how ST can be rejected. McDaniel considers a discontinuous region of space composed of three atomic regions- three collinear points m, n, and p, with m and p one meter apart, and n 0.4 meters from m and 0.6 meters from p. This space region is called Bitty. He states three premises to his counterargument:

1. There are possible worlds in which space is continuous and distance is perfectly natural, external and pervasive.

2. The proper parts of space, which are its sub regions, are contingently existing objects.

3. There are no necessary connections between distinct (non-overlapping) existents.

M, n, and p exist at w (possible world). In w, space is discrete, because the number of spatial atoms is finite. Since the distance relations obtaining between m, n, and p supervene on the intrinsic properties of Bitty, m, n, and p bear the same distance relations to each other as they do in the actual world, although the space which contains them does not exist in the actual world. If ST is true, then, at world w, the distance from m to n equals the distance from n t o p, due to the same number of spatial atoms between m and n and the number of spatial atoms between n and p (both numbers are zero). Nevertheless, the distance from n t o p is greater than the distance from m to n. In w, the distance between two spatial atoms is not just the number of points which the region has as parts, contrary to what is claimed by ST. Therefore, ST is false. By rejecting ST, it is possible to further reject the premises 3 and 4 in the initial argument. McDaniel takes the same approach and shows that the distance between two ending points of the hypotenuse is in fact greater than distances between the points of the sides. Therefore, the physical space cannot be concluded to be not discrete from the set of premises in Weyl's argument.

5) Conclusion

I argued that The Stadium Paradox cannot be solved by rejecting the ATST on the basis of Weyl's tile argument. I introduced the paradox and Weyl's argument which refutes ATST.I showed that when ATST is rejected the paradox can be solved. I used McDaniel's argument against Weyl to explain that the rejection of ATST by using Weyl's theorem does not succeed. While there can be other ways to reject ATST we do not have reasons to do it yet other than the existence of the Stadium paradox itself. I concluded that The Stadium paradox cannot be solved by rejecting ATST on the basis of Weyl's tile argument.
